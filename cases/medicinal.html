The Problem

Centuries of Knowledge, Zero Access

Traditional botanical knowledge about medicinal plants is vast but almost entirely inaccessible, locked in expert memory, dense reference books, and academic databases that everyday users cannot navigate. Meanwhile, people encounter these plants daily in their gardens, parks, and forests with no reliable way to identify them or understand their properties.

The stakes are not academic. Misidentifying a medicinal plant as a safe edible, or misidentifying a toxic plant as a medicinal one, carries real health consequences. The challenge: how do you translate centuries of botanical expertise into an interface that anyone can use correctly in seconds, in the field, possibly without internet access?

Approach

Image Recognition Meets Field UX

The solution combines computer vision (on-device image recognition) with a clean, minimal mobile interface built specifically for field conditions. The user points their camera at a plant; the system identifies it in real time and surfaces the relevant botanical and medicinal information, without requiring an internet connection.

◈

Real-Time Camera Interface

Live viewfinder with visual overlay, bounding boxes and confidence indicators appear as the system processes the image frame by frame. Dr. Sharma sees what the AI is "looking at" and can adjust framing to improve the match.

◉

Botanical Knowledge Layer

Once identified, the app surfaces: common name, scientific name, family, medicinal properties, traditional uses by region, safety classification, toxic lookalikes, and regional availability, in a scannable hierarchy that puts the most critical information first.

◍

IoT Hardware Bridge

Integration with IoT hardware for institutional deployment, field stations, conservation sites, and research institutions can deploy the system on fixed devices in locations where mobile coverage is unreliable. All processing on the edge device.

◎

Offline-First Architecture

Core identification works completely without internet. The botanical database lives on-device. Scan history queues for sync when connectivity returns. For Dr. Sharma 2km into the Western Ghats: zero difference to the experience.

Interface Design

Clarity Under Field Conditions

The interface was designed for three specific environmental realities: bright sunlight that washes out screens, one-handed use while navigating terrain, and the safety-critical nature of the identification task. Every design decision was filtered through these constraints before it was approved.

P1

Clarity, Information Above the Fold

High-contrast UI elements tested for legibility in direct sunlight. The plant's common name, safety classification, and confidence score appear immediately on identification, no scrolling required to get the critical answer. Everything else is secondary, accessed on demand.

P2

Speed, Identification Within 3 Seconds

Results appear within 3 seconds of framing the plant. The UI is completely non-interruptive during scanning, no popups, no loading spinners blocking the viewfinder, no confirmation dialogs. The camera runs continuously while the model processes in the background.

P3

Trust, Visible Uncertainty, Prominent Safety

Confidence scores are visible on every identification, "87% match" vs. "43% match" conveys meaningfully different information. Safety warnings for toxic or ambiguous plants are displayed in a distinct visual system that cannot be mistaken for a regular result. The design never allows overconfidence in an uncertain identification.

Design Decisions
High-contrast color system for sunlight legibility
Large touch targets, gloves-compatible minimum 48px
Critical info above the fold at all times
Distinct visual system for safety-critical alerts

Outcomes & Learnings

Bridging the Knowledge Gap

The project demonstrated that specialised scientific knowledge, typically confined to experts, can be made universally accessible through thoughtful interface design paired with appropriate technology. The hardest design problem wasn't the visual layer; it was deciding how much information to show, in what order, and what to suppress to prevent dangerous overconfidence.

Key Challenges
  • Balancing information density with scannability in field conditions
  • Communicating AI confidence without inducing false certainty
  • Designing safety warnings that are always noticed but never cry-wolf
  • Handling edge cases, partial views, poor lighting, similar-looking species
  • Making the interface work for both the expert (Dr. Sharma) and the novice hiker
Key Learnings
  • Trust UI is as important as accuracy, perception of reliability is a design output
  • Offline-first thinking changes every single design and technical decision you make
  • Field testing is irreplaceable, lab testing missed 60% of the critical use cases
  • Safety-critical UX requires a completely different error-handling philosophy, silence is never an acceptable failure mode
  • A well-designed "I'm not sure" state builds more user trust than a confident wrong answer
"The hardest part of this project wasn't the computer vision. It was designing what to show when the computer vision isn't sure, and making that feel safe."
