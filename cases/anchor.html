The Problem

Money Feels Fake

Young adults are increasingly struggling with financial management due to the "decoupling effect" of digital wallets and credit cards. When you tap a phone instead of handing over cash, money loses its psychological weight, it feels abstract, disconnected from reality. The result: impulsive spending, "zombie subscription" leaks, and a growing cycle of financial anxiety.

Current financial tools make this worse. Mint, YNAB, Rocket Money, they're all reactive. They show you what you've already spent. Historical graphs of your mistakes. Abstract pie charts that don't tell you how to change. Users don't open these apps because they feel like report cards, not tools. The clinical term for this is app avoidance.

KEY FINDING
How might we shift financial apps from reactive reporting to proactive, real-time intervention before a mistake happens?
How might we provide personalized financial guidance without triggering the shame and anxiety that causes users to avoid the app entirely?
How might we make money feel real again when digital payments have stripped away the psychological "pain of paying"?

Research Methodology

24 Participants. Real Stories.

We ran a two-phase research programme, first a literature review and competitive benchmarking to understand the landscape, then direct user engagement through individual interviews and focus groups. All research conducted under IRB Approval #HUM00280873.

P1

Individual Interviews Mental Accounting Habits

8 participants aged 20–32. Semi-structured questions: "What was your last regretful purchase?" and "How do you feel when you open your bank app?" Tools: Zoom for remote sessions, Otter.ai for transcriptions. Signed consent forms from all participants.

P2

Focus Groups Social Spending and Peer Pressure

4 sessions with 4 participants each (16 total). FigJam for digital whiteboarding and real-time clustering. Focused on the role of peer influence on financial decisions, a factor almost completely ignored by existing financial apps.

"For me, it's more the want, the window shopping. If I free up some money, I go spend it on something else. My eyes get big when I see something I want."
"If my friends are going out, I'm going out too. Even if I had considered staying back, I just don't want to be left out."
"I spend more when I'm bored. Surfing online equals unnecessary spending."
"For me, it's juggling everything, bills, assignments, cooking, planning. I can manage money, but I can't manage everything at once here."
"I like when I get positive messages like 'You saved $50 this week!' It makes me want to keep going."
"If an app said 'Everyone slips up sometimes' instead of scolding you, it'd feel encouraging."

I1

The "Pain of Paying" is Dead

Digital wallets make money feel fake. Requirement: Visual Anchors that translate dollars into real-world units, hours of work, coffee equivalents, concrete comparisons that reconnect spending to reality.

I2

Graphs Cause Stress

Complex charts lead directly to app avoidance. Requirement: Conversational UI, a chat-based AI assistant that feels like a supportive coach, not a financial dashboard judging your choices.

I3

Social Pressure is the #1 Budget Killer

"If my friends are going out, I'm going out too." Requirement: Peer and group goal tracking, tools to navigate social spending without shame or isolation.

Usability Testing

Two Rounds. Real Iterations.

We used Maze (integrated with our Figma prototype) and Zoom to run two rounds of moderated usability testing with 16 total participants. Four core tasks per session, think-aloud protocol throughout. After Round 1, we redesigned. Then tested again.

Round 1 Key Findings
  • Task 1 (Explore App): 22.2% success, 130.3s avg time, 29% misclick rate, unclear icons and labels
  • Task 2 (AI Insights): 87.5% success, helpful but hard to locate due to navigation issues
  • Tasks 3 + 4 (Budget + AI Shopping): 100% success, but 32.1% and 8.5% misclick rates
  • Users wanted negative budget scenarios and more flexible fund transfer options
  • AI chat bubble was easy to miss, not visually distinct from the nav bar
Round 2 Improvements Validated
  • Task 1 (Explore App): 100% success, 0% misclick, simplified home screen worked
  • Task 2 (AI Chat Bubble): 100% success, but 9.1% misclick still showed discoverability gap
  • Task 3 (Budget Adjust): 87.5% success but 54.7% misclick rate, flow still needed work
  • Users reported high ease of use (4.4/5), but data showed a discoverability gap between perceived and actual usability
  • 75% completely trust the AI recommendations after seeing explanations
Round 1: Home page + AI Anchor placement
Round 2: Budget flow + AI chat bubble visibility
AI transparency: surfacing the "why" behind recommendations

Conclusion & Learnings

What We Proved. What We Learned.

Money Anchor successfully demonstrated that a shift from reactive reporting to proactive, AI-driven intervention is both technically feasible and genuinely preferred by users. Usability testing showed high task success and positive sentiment, especially for AI recommendations and budgeting features, while surfacing real challenges in discoverability and initial AI trust.

100%
Task success rate on core flows in Round 2
4.4/5
Perceived ease of use (Round 2 participant rating)
75%
Users completely trust AI recommendations after seeing explanations
2×
Rounds of testing, with full prototype iteration between rounds
L1

Perceived Usability ≠ Actual Usability

Users reported ease (4.4/5) while simultaneously struggling with feature discoverability. The gap between self-reported and measured usability is real, you need both qualitative feedback and quantitative Maze data to see the full picture.

L2

Visibility is Critical

The AI Anchor, our most important feature, was missed by users in Round 1 because it looked like part of the nav bar. Important features must be visually distinct. A 54.7% misclick rate on budget adjustment in Round 2 reinforced this: users can't use what they can't find.

L3

Trust in AI Requires Transparency

Users prefer explanations, control, and optional suggestions, not prescriptive actions. The "why behind the recommendation" is as important as the recommendation itself. Once we surfaced AI reasoning clearly, trust jumped to 75% complete confidence.

L4

Iterative Testing Drives Better Design

Two rounds of testing uncovered issues a single round would have missed. Prototype early, test often, and treat every misclick as a design question, not a user error.
