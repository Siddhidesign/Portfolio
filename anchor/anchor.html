Academic Context

This is an IRB-approved (#HUM00280873) capstone research project at the University of Michigan-Dearborn, Masters of Science in Human Centered Design & Engineering, advised by Professor Sang-Hwan Kim. Prototypes are in active development as of 2025–2026.

Challenge

Young adults struggle with financial awareness due to the "decoupling effect" of digital payments — spending feels less real, leading to impulsive behavior, subscription blindness, and financial anxiety.

Solution

An AI-driven mobile budgeting app that provides proactive, non-judgmental guidance — translating numbers into real-life meaning and building long-term financial habits through transparent AI recommendations.

Impact

Shifted financial tools from reactive tracking to proactive intervention, reduced cognitive overload through simplified data visualization, and increased user trust through transparent AI recommendations.

01

The problem

Young adults in their 20s and early 30s face a unique financial challenge — digital payments have decoupled the emotional weight of spending. Swiping a card or tapping a phone feels fundamentally different from handing over cash. The result: spending feels less real, decisions are more impulsive, and financial anxiety grows quietly in the background.

Research Methodology

To understand how young adults think and feel about money, I led 8 one-on-one interviews and 4 focus groups totaling 24 participants across ages 20–32. All research was IRB approved.

🎙️

User Interviews

8 individual sessions with participants aged 20–32. Conducted over Zoom, transcribed via Otter.ai. Each session lasted 45–60 minutes exploring spending behavior, emotional triggers, and tool usage patterns.

Focus Groups

4 focus groups with 16 total participants. Groups explored shared financial habits and peer influences on spending. Participants discussed real transactions and emotional reactions to budgeting tools.

7 Key Research Findings

1

Lack of real-time spending awareness

Users only noticed overspending after the fact — notifications came too late to change behavior in the moment.

2

Impulsive and emotionally driven spending

Boredom, stress, and social influence were the primary triggers for unplanned purchases — not deliberate financial decisions.

3

"Subscription blindness"

Invisible recurring payments were accumulating unnoticed — users knew they existed but rarely audited them proactively.

4

Preference for simplicity

Complex dashboards caused users to avoid the app entirely. Any friction caused abandonment — the bar for engagement was extremely low.

5

Need for a non-judgmental, supportive tone

Users wanted a coach, not a critic. Negative framing of overspending created shame and active disengagement from the tool.

6

Trust in AI only with transparency

Users were open to AI recommendations — but only if they understood the reasoning behind them. Opaque suggestions felt arbitrary.

7

Need for contextual money understanding

Raw numbers were meaningless without context. Users needed spending translated into real-life equivalents to understand impact.

03

The User

Research surfaced two distinct user archetypes — defined not by demographics, but by their relationship with financial anxiety and control. Both needed support, but in very different ways.

The Anxious Avoider

Persona 01

Ages 22–28
Avoids financial tools

Goal

  • Feel in control without feeling overwhelmed
  • Engage with finances on their own terms — low pressure
  • Receive encouragement, not warning messages

Pain Points

  • Interacts minimally with financial tools due to stress and fear of judgment
  • Avoids opening the app after overspending — enters a shame loop
  • Complex dashboards trigger anxiety instead of providing clarity
"I know I need to budget but every time I open these apps I feel worse about myself. I need something that doesn't make me feel like I'm failing."

The Precision Planner

Persona 02

Ages 25–32
Active finance manager

Goal

  • Optimize every dollar with data-driven decisions
  • Access predictive insights and forward-looking projections
  • Understand the "why" behind every AI recommendation

Pain Points

  • Current tools lack predictive capabilities — they only show history
  • AI recommendations feel arbitrary without any explanation
  • No way to model different financial scenarios
"I track everything already — I just want the app to think one step ahead of me. Show me where I'm heading, not just where I've been."

05

Key App Screens

Five core screens were designed to map directly to the top research needs — each solving a specific pain point surfaced during user interviews.

🏠

Home Page — Budget

Color-coded budget tiles give users an instant at-a-glance view of their spending status. Green means on track; amber means approaching limit; red means over — no need to read numbers to understand status.

Addresses: Lack of real-time awareness · Preference for simplicity

🏦

Accounts

Connected bank accounts and balance overview in one consolidated view. Eliminates the need to switch between banking apps to understand total financial health at a glance.

Addresses: Subscription blindness · Contextual money understanding

🤖

AI Assistant

Conversational AI that delivers personalized recommendations with transparent reasoning. Every suggestion includes a brief "why" — building user trust over time. Tone is coach-like, never critical.

Addresses: Trust in AI · Non-judgmental tone · Contextual understanding

💳

Transactions

Auto-categorized spending history that organizes every transaction without manual input. Users can review, correct categories, and understand their spending patterns at a glance.

Addresses: AI auto-categorization · Real-time spending awareness

📊

Spending Report

Visual trend analysis showing spending patterns over time — not just totals. Users can see where money is consistently going and identify opportunities to adjust before the next billing cycle.

Addresses: Spending trend visualization · Predictive financial outlook

06

Evaluation Results

Two rounds of usability testing were conducted to validate the design and measure the impact of iterations between rounds.

ROUND 1 — Initial Testing

Findings & Friction Points

  • Navigation between screens was unclear to first-time users
  • AI assistant tone needed adjustment — felt too formal in places
  • Color-coded tiles needed a clearer legend and explanation
  • Budget creation flow had too many steps, causing drop-off
ROUND 2 — Post-Iteration

Improvements Observed

  • Improved task completion rate across key flows
  • Reduced cognitive load through simplified data views
  • Users responded positively to non-judgmental AI tone
  • Budget creation streamlined — reduced friction and confusion

08

What did I learn?

1. Behavioral design is as important as visual design

Emotions drive financial decisions. Designing for behavioral change — not just information display — required understanding psychology as much as interface conventions. The "decoupling effect" wasn't a visual problem; it was a behavioral one.

2. Simplicity requires more work

Distilling complex financial data into clear, actionable insights is harder than showing everything. Every screen required deliberate choices about what to hide as much as what to show. The hardest design work was in the editing.

3. AI trust is earned through transparency

Users were open to AI recommendations — but only when they understood the reasoning. "Here's what I recommend" is not enough. "Here's why I recommend this" is what builds lasting trust. This shaped every AI interaction we designed.

09 Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

Three honest things I'd change — not as self-criticism, but as a record of what this project taught me about research-driven design.

1

I'd include more diverse age groups beyond 20–32 in research

All 24 participants fell within the 20–32 age range. Financial behaviors change significantly across life stages — a 35-year-old with dependents has fundamentally different challenges than a 22-year-old fresh out of college. Broadening the research lens would have surfaced use cases we missed, and strengthened the universality of our design decisions.

2

I'd test the subscription detection feature earlier in the process

Subscription detection was one of the highest-rated features in our ideation scoring — yet we didn't prototype and test it until later in the cycle. Given its complexity (linking to bank accounts, parsing recurring transactions, providing cancellation flows), earlier testing would have surfaced technical constraints that affected its final design. It's a lesson in prioritizing prototyping by risk, not just by excitement.

3

I'd explore more ways to make the AI feel conversational and less robotic

Users reacted well to the non-judgmental tone in testing, but several noted the AI still felt "text-book" in places. With more time, I would have explored richer conversational patterns — personalized greetings, contextual responses, and language that adapted to whether a user was on track or struggling. The gap between "not judgmental" and "genuinely supportive" is where the real design work lives.

10

Conclusion

Thank you for reading through this case study. Money Anchor challenged me to think about design at the intersection of behavioral psychology and financial technology. The most meaningful shift this project produced wasn't a specific feature — it was the reframing of budgeting apps from reactive tools that track what happened, to proactive systems that help users shape what happens next.

This project is ongoing as part of my capstone. I'm continuing to refine the prototype, test with new participants, and work toward a fully documented research publication with the team at University of Michigan-Dearborn.
